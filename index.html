Making an FOI Request

Freedom of Information — Commonwealth 5 stones · 1 keystone · 2 choices
1
Identify your right Right Foundation
You have a legal right to access documents held by Commonwealth agencies. This is the foundation stone — everything else rests on it. No citizenship, residency, or special qualifications required.
This right comes from the Freedom of Information Act 1982 (Cth), section 11. It exists for everyone.
2
Prepare your request
Write a request that describes what you're looking for. You don't need file names — describe the topic, time period, and the kinds of documents they might be. Think subject and date range.
A reliable starting pattern: "All correspondence, briefing notes, and minutes about [your topic] between [start date] and [end date]."
3
Lodge with the agency Keystone · 30 days
This is the keystone moment. Submitting your request to the agency starts the 30-day statutory clock. Once this stone is placed, the arch holds — the agency is bound by law to respond.
The agency may try to narrow your request (practical refusal, s 24) or consult third parties (s 26A). These are part of the process — they don't remove the stone, they just adjust its load.
4
Agency decision Decision point
The agency decides whether to grant full access, partial access with exemption-based redactions, or refuse access. Each exemption (ss 33–47C) must be individually justified.
Look for the schedule of documents — it lists every document identified, the decision made on each, and which exemption applies. This is your map of the agency's reasoning.
5
Review options Choice point
If the decision doesn't satisfy, three escalating routes exist. Each re-examines the original decision from a different height in the arch.
Internal review (same agency, fresh eyes) Information Commissioner review Administrative Appeals Tribunal
